01 — The Energy Problem
The Fundamental Inefficiency of Dense Representations
State-of-the-art language models encode every token as a dense vector in which all dimensions are active. This density is computationally expensive, memory-intensive, and may be fundamentally unnecessary. We proved that language concentrates into 8 of 64 frequency bins — 87.5% of the spectrum carries less than 0.2% of the information.
✓ Measured
Parameter Efficiency at 1B Scale
50M encoder on OpenWebText. SparseWave spectral embeddings (top-k bins) vs standard dense embeddings on identical downstream transformers.
⚡ 110K parameters at PPL 1.024 versus 65.2M parameters at PPL 2.719 — same task, same data, same evaluation.
↗ Projected
FLOPS Reduction at Industry Scale
Dimension-dependent operations (QKV projections, FFN layers) scale as O(d²). Reducing effective dimensionality by 87.5% cuts these FLOPS proportionally.
📐 Based on measured k=8/k=64 perplexity ratio of 1.002. Projection assumes sparsity holds at larger model dimensions.
02 — Superposition
A Capability Dense Representations Cannot Replicate
Adding two token embeddings produces an unrecoverable mixture. Adding two waveforms produces a composite signal from which both originals can be recovered — a property of linear signal superposition. This enables constant-memory knowledge storage.
✓ Measured
Full-Pipeline Superposition Decode
Encode N documents → sum into one waveform → subtract N-1 → decode through GPT-2. Measure word overlap with original.
🔬 At N=20 superimposed documents: 95.7% average word overlap — identical to the N=1 baseline. Zero degradation through the full pipeline.
↗ Projected
Memory Scaling: Linear vs Constant
Traditional storage grows linearly. SparseWave superposition maintains a constant-size carrier signal regardless of document count.
💾 Measured: 1,000 documents on a single carrier at 99.68% accuracy. 10,000 documents at 93.6%.
03 — Scaling Behavior
Spectral Sparsity Tightens With Capacity
Most compression techniques degrade at scale. SparseWave's spectral sparsity does the opposite — it tightens with larger encoders. The k=8/k=64 perplexity ratio dropped from 1.14 to 1.002 as encoder capacity increased from 1M to 50M parameters.
✓ Measured

k=8/k=64 PPL ratio at three encoder sizes. 1.0 = perfect sparsity (8 bins carry all information).

04 — Controlled Ablation
FFT Encoding Is Not Cosmetic
Same architecture, same data, same compute, same hyperparameters. The only variable: one encoder uses an FFT pathway, the other uses a standard learned linear projection. The FFT encoder converges to half the validation loss.
✓ Measured — Test 1
Validation Loss: FFT Encoder vs Plain Encoder
v3.7 (FFT) ran 48 epochs to convergence. Test 1 (plain) at epoch 12, still improving but already 2× behind. Both trained from scratch on identical data.
🧪 FFT converges at val 1.53. Plain encoder at val 3.14 after 12 epochs — projected plateau ~2.5. The frequency domain learns language structure faster and more completely.
