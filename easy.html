What is SparseWave?

We discovered that AI is doing way more work than it needs to. Here's what that means.

🧠 The Problem with AI Today

Every time ChatGPT writes a sentence, it does billions of math operations per word. Every single number in the model is active for every single calculation.

Imagine you had to use every muscle in your body just to pick up a cup of coffee. That's essentially what AI is doing right now — using everything, all the time, for everything.

The question nobody was asking: does it actually need all that?

🔬 What We Found

We developed a new way to represent language inside AI systems. When we looked at these representations, we found something striking: most of the representation is empty.

Think of it like a warehouse

Current AI uses a massive warehouse to store the meaning of every word — and every shelf is full, every slot is used.

We found a way to reorganize that warehouse. When we did, it turned out almost all the shelves could be empty. The actual information fits in a small corner. The rest was just noise the AI was carrying around for no reason.

64 representation channels — what actually carries information:

Only ~8 out of 64 channels carry real information

Out of 64 channels in our representation, only about 8 actually carry meaningful information. The other 56 are basically silent. And when we threw away those silent channels? The AI worked just as well.

We tested this on 1 billion tokens of real internet text — news articles, blog posts, forum discussions. Not cherry-picked examples. Real, messy, diverse language.

The result: a tiny model with 110,000 parameters using our encoding beat a standard model with 65 million parameters. Same data, same task — 590× smaller, better results.

💡 Why This Matters

The highway analogy

Imagine a 64-lane highway where only 8 lanes have cars on them. The other 56 lanes are empty, but you're still paying to maintain all 64.

Every AI company in the world is paying to maintain all 64 lanes. We proved you only need 8. Same traffic, fraction of the cost.

🤯 The Surprising Part

Here's what surprised us most: we didn't force the efficiency.

We never told the system "use fewer channels." We just built the representation and let it train. It chose to concentrate everything into a handful of channels on its own.

Even more surprising: when we made the model bigger, it didn't spread out and use more channels. It used fewer. The efficiency got tighter.

What this tells us

The information content of human language is intrinsically compact when viewed through the right lens. Current AI systems are vastly over-provisioned — not because they need to be, but because nobody had found the right way to look at the problem.

We found a new way to look at it. And it changes the math significantly.

❓ Common Questions

How is this different from just compressing AI models?

Most AI compression (pruning, quantization) takes a big model and makes it smaller after the fact — like shrinking a photo. We're changing how the model sees language in the first place. Instead of compressing a dense representation, we discovered that language has a naturally sparse structure when represented correctly. It's like discovering the photo was mostly blank to begin with.

Could this make ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini faster?

Potentially, yes. If the core insight holds at larger scales — that language only needs a fraction of the representation bandwidth AI currently uses — then models could operate on much smaller internal representations. That means less computation per word, which means faster and cheaper. There's engineering work between a research finding and a production system, but the fundamental efficiency gain is real.

Has anyone else done this before?

No. Others have worked on making AI models more efficient through compression, pruning, and clever engineering. But nobody has demonstrated that language representations are inherently sparse in the way we've shown — or achieved this kind of parameter efficiency gap (590×) on real-world data at scale.
