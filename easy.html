What is SparseWave?

We discovered that AI is doing way more math than it needs to. Here's what that means.

🧠 The Problem with AI Today

Every time ChatGPT writes a sentence, it does billions of math operations per word. Every single dimension of every single number is active for every single calculation.

Imagine you had to use every muscle in your body just to pick up a cup of coffee. That's essentially what AI is doing right now — using everything, all the time, for everything.

The question nobody was asking: does it actually need all that?

🔬 What We Found

We converted language into sound waves using math (Fourier transforms — the same math that makes your music sound right on Spotify). Then we looked at the waves.

Think of it like a music equalizer

When you look at an equalizer on a stereo, you see different frequency bars — bass, mids, treble. Some bars are tall, most are short.

We built an equalizer for language. And when we looked at it, almost all the bars were flat. Language only uses a tiny handful of frequencies.

Only ~8 out of 64 bins carry real information

Out of 64 frequency channels, only about 8 actually carry meaningful information. The other 56 are basically silent. And when we threw away those silent channels? The AI worked just as well.

🤯 The Surprising Part

Here's what blew our minds: we didn't force the sparsity.

We never told the AI "use fewer frequencies." We just gave it the ability to use frequencies and trained it to reconstruct text. It chose to concentrate everything into a handful of bins on its own.

Even more surprising: when we made the encoder bigger (50 million parameters instead of 1 million), it didn't spread out and use more frequencies. It used fewer. The sparsity got tighter.

What this tells us

Language itself has a natural "frequency fingerprint." It's not something we're imposing — it's something language is. The information content of human language is intrinsically low-dimensional when viewed through the right lens.

❓ Common Questions

Is this actually turning text into sound?

Sort of! We use the same math (Fourier transforms) that converts sound between waveforms and frequencies. The text becomes a real waveform — you could technically play it through a speaker, though it wouldn't sound like anything meaningful to human ears. The point is the mathematical structure, not the audio.

How is this different from just compressing AI models?

Most AI compression (pruning, quantization) takes a big model and makes it smaller after the fact — like shrinking a photo. We're changing what the model looks at in the first place. Instead of compressing a dense representation, we discovered that language has a naturally sparse representation in the frequency domain. It's like discovering the photo was mostly blank to begin with.

Could this make ChatGPT/Claude faster?

Potentially, yes. If the core insight holds at larger scales — that language only uses a fraction of available frequency bandwidth — then AI models could operate on much smaller representations. That means less math per word, which means faster and cheaper. But there's a lot of engineering between a research finding and a production system.

Has anyone else done this before?

People have used Fourier transforms inside AI models before (Google's FNet, Fourier Neural Operators), but always as a computational trick — a faster way to do the same thing. Nobody has used the frequency domain as the representation itself and discovered that language is naturally sparse in it. That's the new part.
